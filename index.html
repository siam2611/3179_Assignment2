Population Impact on YouTube

This data-driven exploration seeks to shed light on the fascinating connection between population and key YouTube statistics such as earnings, subscribers, and the number of YouTube channels. We present a dual perspective: on the left side, you'll find absolute data, showing the direct impact of population on these metrics. On the right, we've normalised the data, allowing readers to make fair comparisons across countries of varying sizes. The dataset used was obtained from Kaggle (author: NIDULA ELGIRIYEWITHAN).

Visualisation created by Siam Ahmed

The scatterplot depicts a positive correlation between population and revenue, with countries like India and the United States illustrating this trend. However, notable outliers including China, Peru, Venezuela, and Bangladesh challenge this pattern. China's restriction of platforms like YouTube, unique economic structure, and wealth disparities may explain its position. Peru's reliance on volatile industries and regional disparities may contribute to its outlier status. Venezuela's economic crisis and political instability play a pivotal role in its low revenue despite oil wealth, while Bangladesh's underdeveloped economic structure and agrarian economy could clarify its position. It is essential to consider factors like economic structure, political stability, and global economic conditions alongside population in understanding a country's revenue.

This scatterplot, depicting the relationship between population, total earnings, and earnings per capita, reveals insightful nuances in economic performance. Notably, countries like Barbados and Latvia, with smaller populations, exhibit large circle sizes, indicating robust earnings per capita and efficient resource utilisation. In contrast, India, despite its substantial total earnings, presents a smaller circle size, highlighting the challenge of income distribution among a large population. This underscores the significance of normalisation when assessing a country's economic well-being and emphasises the pivotal role of population size in shaping a nation's earnings, reinforcing the need for a nuanced approach to cross-country comparisons.

This choropleth graph effectively illustrates the distribution of subscribers by country, with dark blue indicating high subscriber counts and green representing lower counts, while yellow denotes countries with minimal or unavailable data. Notably, countries such as the United States, India, Brazil, and South Korea are characterised by dark blue, signifying substantial subscriber numbers and a general trend that correlates larger populations with higher subscriber counts. Nevertheless, it's important to acknowledge that this relationship is not uniform across all nations, as other factors like economic development, technological infrastructure, and cultural influences play a role. The presence of countries in yellow suggests data limitations or exceptionally low subscription rates, influenced by various socio-economic and political factors. This graph provides a valuable snapshot of global subscription patterns, indicating both population-driven trends and regional disparities.

This choropleth graph, now normalised for subscribers per capita, reveals significant shifts in the colour scheme and provides a fairer basis for comparing countries' subscription patterns. India and Brazil, previously depicted in dark blue, now appear in a lighter blue shade, signifying a lower subscribers-to-population ratio. In contrast, China, which was originally light blue, transitions to yellow, indicating a notably lower subscribers-per-capita ratio. The United States and South Korea maintain their original colours, emphasising their robust performance in terms of subscribers per capita. This normalisation underscores the importance of considering population size when assessing subscription patterns, allowing for a more equitable and insightful comparison among countries. It demonstrates that a high subscriber count alone does not necessarily equate to a strong subscription rate per capita, highlighting the need for nuanced analysis to gauge the true impact of digital services across different nations.

The area chart portraying the evolution of channels in continents from 2005 to 2022 highlights the connection between population size and channel growth. Asia and North America exhibit substantial areas, mirroring their large populations and robust digital infrastructure, leading to an increase in channels over the years. Conversely, Oceania's smaller population is reflected in its comparatively modest area on the chart. However, Africa stands out as an exception with its significant population but a smaller area, attributable to factors such as limited technology access, economic challenges, political instability, and linguistic and cultural diversity. This anomaly underscores the intricate interplay of various factors, beyond population alone, in shaping the media landscape across continents.

When examining the area chart displaying channels per 100 million people from 2005 to 2022, a notable shift in trends becomes evident, emphasising the importance of data normalisation for equitable comparisons. Asia, which previously exhibited a substantial area in the chart, now appears as the second smallest after Africa. This change indicates that, when adjusted for population size, Asia's growth in channels per capita is not as significant as previously suggested. Conversely, North America maintains a large area, highlighting its continued strength in terms of channels per 100 million people. Oceania experiences a substantial increase in area, suggesting significant growth in channels per capita, likely influenced by improved digital infrastructure. This normalisation underscores the necessity of considering population size when evaluating media landscape trends, as it allows for a more equitable comparison among continents, revealing nuanced patterns that might otherwise be obscured.
